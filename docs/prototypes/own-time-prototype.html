Session 2 — Sketching

In Progress
Welcome back, Alex! Last session you found that hiking backpack article about load distribution — great find. You said you'd start sketching today. Ready to get into it?
Yeah, I've got some ideas in my head but I'm not sure how to get them on paper. My sketches always look rubbish.
That's completely normal — every designer feels that way at first. Here's the thing: these aren't presentation drawings. They're thinking tools. A rough sketch that captures your idea is more useful than a beautiful drawing with no substance. Start with this: draw your strap from the side view. Where does it connect to the bag? Where does it sit on the shoulder? How wide is it? Don't worry about straight lines — focus on the key decisions.
Ok I drew one. It's got a wider padded section where it sits on the shoulder and then it narrows where it connects to the bag. Kind of like how hiking backpacks do it but thinner.
Smart starting point — widening at the shoulder distributes force over a larger area, which is exactly what your research showed. Now here's the question that'll push your design further: you said "kind of like hiking backpacks but thinner." What makes a school bag different from a hiking backpack in terms of how people actually wear it? That difference should change your design.
Hmm. I guess with school bags you're constantly taking it on and off. Like every class. And you usually only use one shoulder half the time even though you're not supposed to.
Now THAT is a real insight. The on/off frequency and the one-shoulder problem are constraints that hiking packs don't have. What if your strap design specifically addressed one-shoulder carrying? Not by preventing it — students will always do it — but by making it less damaging when they do?
That's actually a cool idea. Like what if the strap could slide to centre when you go one-shoulder? So the weight isn't all on the edge of your shoulder.
That's your best idea so far — a strap that adapts to how students actually carry bags rather than how they're "supposed to." Sketch that. Show the strap in both positions: two-shoulder and one-shoulder with the slide mechanism. What would the sliding part look like physically?

Session 1 — Mon 17 Mar
Research & Discovery
Researched ergonomic backpack design principles. Found a key article on hiking backpack engineering that explained load distribution through wide, contoured straps. Noted that school bags face a different problem — constant on/off and one-shoulder carrying that hiking packs don't have to solve for.
Research synthesis Source evaluation
"I learned that most school bags are designed wrong — they assume you'll always use both straps, but nobody does. I want to design for how students actually carry bags."
Session 2 — Wed 19 Mar (Today)
Concept Sketching
Developed 3 concept sketches for strap designs. Initial sketches were direct copies of hiking backpack straps. Through mentor discussion, identified the key insight: school bag straps should adapt to one-shoulder carrying rather than fighting it. Concept 3 features a sliding mechanism that recentres the strap when carried on one shoulder.
Concept development Design thinking Sketching
"My first two sketches were boring — just copies of what already exists. The sliding strap idea came when the mentor asked me to think about how students ACTUALLY carry bags. That's the concept I want to prototype."

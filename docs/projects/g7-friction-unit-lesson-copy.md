# G7 Friction Unit — Class 1 & 2 student-facing copy

Drop-in copy for the first two lessons. Paste into the lesson editor
or use as a verbal script. Designed to set up the problem-first
framing without giving away the form-emergence-from-problem move
(that's the *learning*, not the *briefing*).

---

## Class 1 — Notice & Sort

### Lesson hook (1 paragraph, top of lesson page)

Before designers make anything, they *notice*. They watch how people
actually live, work, eat, walk, carry things — and they pay attention
to the small frictions that everyone has accepted as normal. The
broken-but-tolerated stuff. The "I just deal with it" stuff. That's
where good design starts. Today we're going to practise noticing.

### What's a friction? (intro card)

A friction is a small breakdown in everyday life that someone has
just learned to live with. It's not a disaster. It's not a
philosophical problem. It's a *workaround* you can see in the wild.

**Good frictions:**
- People drop their phones when carrying too many books.
- The basil dies every time we go on holiday.
- My grandmother can't open the salsa jar.

**Not-quite-frictions** (we'll come back to these later):
- Climate change. *(Too big — no one person experiences it as a
  daily moment.)*
- My brother is annoying. *(Too vague — what specifically? When?
  What workaround do you use?)*
- I want to make a board game. *(That's a solution, not a friction.
  What friction does the board game solve?)*

Friction = a *real moment* where a *real person* has invented a
*workaround*. If you can spot the workaround, you've found a
friction worth investigating.

### Today's task (in-class, 15 min)

In pairs, list 5 frictions you've noticed in the last 24 hours.
For each one, answer:

1. Who experiences it? (one specific kind of person, not "everyone")
2. When does it happen? (morning? evening? at school? at home?)
3. What workaround have they invented? (the tell that it's a real
   friction)

### Homework (between Class 1 and Class 2)

Over the next 24 hours, photograph **5 frictions** you notice. Yours
or someone else's. Phone photo is fine — it's the *noticing* that
matters, not the photography.

Each photo: one sentence in the caption — *who*, *when*, *what
workaround*. Upload to the lesson page before next class.

## Class 2 — Share & Pick

### Lesson hook (top of lesson page)

You walked around for 24 hours noticing frictions. Today you pick
one. Not to *solve* yet — to *investigate*. This becomes the
question your unit is built around.

### Share-out (15 min, table groups)

In groups of 4, share your 5 photos. As you go, listen for two
things:

- **Surprising frictions** — ones you hadn't thought about before.
- **Recurring frictions** — ones multiple people noticed
  independently. (That's a signal it's real.)

Each group picks the *most interesting* friction from their pile
(not necessarily the most important — interesting). One person
reports out to the class.

### The Choice Cards (the picker block)

We've prepped 9 cards covering frictions a lot of G7-aged designers
have found interesting. Read them. If yours matches one of these,
pick that card. If yours doesn't match anything here, use **"Pitch
your own friction"** and write yours up.

> **Read the cards. Pick one to investigate.**
>
> You're not committing to making anything yet. You're committing
> to a *friction worth understanding*. Next class you'll go talk to
> someone who actually experiences it.

### What happens next (briefing for Class 3)

Next class:
- Each of you will pick **one person** who experiences your chosen
  friction.
- You'll prepare 5 interview questions. They have to be **open
  questions about the experience**, not solution-pitches.
- Bad question: *"Would you use an app that reminds you of stuff?"*
  (That's a solution disguised as a question.)
- Good question: *"Tell me about the last time you forgot something
  important. What happened? What did you wish was different?"*

You'll interview your person between Class 2 and Class 3 (10-15
minutes — phone call, in person, whatever works).

### Important reminder

The friction you pick is the *start* of your investigation, not the
*end*. By the time we get to designing solutions in a few classes,
you might have completely changed your mind about what the real
friction is — and that's the point. Designers update.

## Teacher notes (not student-facing)

### Class 1 things to watch for

- **Scope Chasm in observations.** If a student lists "climate
  change" or "school is unfair" — push them to "what's the *moment*
  this happens?" Make them concrete. They can't take a photo of
  unfairness; they can take a photo of one person waiting in a long
  line.
- **Solutions disguised as frictions.** "I want to design a smart
  water bottle" is a solution. Push: "what friction does it solve?
  What do you see *that bottle owners do badly* today?"
- **Photo gimmickry.** The point isn't the photos. If a student
  comes back with 5 staged photos and no captions, the noticing
  didn't happen.

### Class 2 things to watch for

- **Trojan Horse picks.** Listen during share-out: is the student
  describing the friction, or already describing a product? "I picked
  forgotten stuff because I want to make an app" = Trojan Horse.
  Redirect: "Pick the friction. The form will come later."
- **Anti-pattern interview questions** to flag explicitly: *"Would
  you use ___?"* / *"Don't you think ___ would be better?"* / *"How
  often do you use ___?"* (Anything that names a product.)
- **Pro-pattern interview questions** to model: *"Tell me about the
  last time..."* / *"Walk me through what happened..."* / *"What
  did you wish was different in that moment?"* / *"What do other
  people do that surprises you?"*

### Success signal for Class 3

By end of Class 3, count: **how many students report a surprising
root cause** they didn't know in Class 1? (Example: thought mornings
were chaotic because of sleep, learned it's actually the shared
mirror.)

Target: **≥ 20%** = framing is working. <20% = need to scaffold
Empathy harder before re-testing.

Failure-mode tells from Class 3 interviews:
- *Trojan Horse* — student is pitching a solution in the interview.
- *Scope Chasm* — student couldn't find a real person to interview.

C02 Racers
Now is the time to get some of those race-car design ideas — undoubtedly floating around in your head — on paper. The best way to start is with concept sketches, also known as thumbnail sketches. Thumbnails are small, quick sketches used by engineers and designers to rapidly communicate ideas. They should not be detailed.  Be sure to experiment with different ideas and be as creative as possible. See the next slides for things to keep in mind and examples…

Task 2: Ideation
The Objective
Goal:
 To generate 5 unique and creative design concepts for your CO2 dragster.
Why?
 The first idea is rarely the best idea. By exploring different shapes, aerodynamics, and structures, you ensure your final choice is the fastest and most efficient design possible.
Constraint Checklist:
 All 5 designs must adhere to the technical regulations (e.g., minimum length, axle hole placement, CO2 cartridge housing safety).

Required Elements for Each Sketch:
 
Side View (Profile):
 Shows aerodynamics and height.
Top View (Plan):
 Shows width and body shape.
Annotations:
 Notes explaining your thinking (e.g., "Tapered back for reduced drag," "Hollow body for weight reduction").

Marking Criteria
Achievement Level
Descriptor
Specific Indicators for this Task
0
The student does not reach a standard described by any of the descriptors below.
No work submitted or work does not relate to CO2 cars.
1–2
Basic
 
The student develops a 
limited
 range of design ideas. The ideas can be interpreted by others with 
difficulty
.
• Draws fewer than 3 concepts.
• Sketches are messy, lacking detail, or missing views (e.g., only side view).
• Designs look very similar to each other.
• Very few or no annotations explaining the design features.
3–4
Developing
 
The student develops a range of 
feasible
 design ideas. The ideas can be interpreted by others.
• Draws 3-4 distinct concepts.
• Sketches are clear enough to understand the general shape.
• Includes side and top views for most designs.
• Basic annotations are present (e.g., "fast shape," "lightweight").
• Designs generally follow the rules but may have minor feasibility issues.
5–6
Competent
 
The student develops a range of 
feasible
 design ideas. The ideas can be 
correctly interpreted
 by others. The student presents the chosen design and outlines the reasons for its selection.
• Draws 5 distinct concepts.
• Sketches are neat and include clear Side and Top views.
• Annotations use some technical terminology (drag, mass).
• Designs are clearly different from one another.
• It is clear how the car would be built based on the drawing.
7–8
Excellent
 
The student develops a range of 
feasible
 design ideas. The ideas can be 
correctly interpreted
 by others. The student presents the chosen design and 
justifies
 its selection.
• Draws 5 highly distinct and creative concepts.
• Sketches are high quality, perhaps including isometric views or colour coding.
• Annotations are detailed, linking specific design features to scientific principles (e.g., "Tapered rear to reduce flow separation").
• All designs strictly adhere to the technical regulations (feasibility).
• Clear evidence of "thinking on paper."

# Cowork response — CO2 Racers agency unit research

> **Provenance:** Cowork prompt + response, 5 May 2026. Saved verbatim as the third pedagogical lens (alongside Claude's lead synthesis + Gemini's earlier response). Cited by `co2-racers-agency-unit.md`.

Five questions, in order. Where the research is robust I'll cite it; where it's thin (especially WIP limits with 12-year-olds) I'll say so plainly rather than invent.

## 1. Adolescent agency — what's measurable, what's proxy

The most useful frame is **Bandura's four-component agentic perspective** — intentionality, forethought, self-reactiveness, self-reflectiveness (Bandura, 2006) — layered with **Self-Determination Theory** (Deci & Ryan): agency emerges where autonomy, competence, and relatedness are simultaneously supported. At 12-13, the prefrontal cortex is undergoing the executive-function consolidation that makes forethought and self-reactiveness *possible* but not *reliable*, while identity formation is accelerating (Erikson, Marcia). This is the developmental sweet spot for *teaching* agency — earlier and the metacognitive capacity isn't there; later and the habits of compliance have already set.

For maker contexts specifically, **Project Zero's Agency by Design** (Clapp, Tishman, Ross, et al., *Maker-Centered Learning*, 2017) names three maker capacities — *looking closely*, *exploring complexity*, *finding opportunity* — and argues that agency in making is downstream of "sensitivity to design," a perceptual disposition. Their thinking routines (Parts/Purposes/Complexities; Imagine If; Think/Feel/Care) are explicitly designed to make agency observable.

**Real evidence of growing agency in your context:**

- *Initiated revision* — changes a wheel design without being told to, and can articulate *why* (not "it broke" but "it broke because the axle hole was off-centre")
- *Calibrated help-seeking* — asks specific questions ("how do I deal with grain tear-out on the back face?") not generic ones ("can you help?"). Help-seeking is itself an agency behaviour (Newman, 2002)
- *Trade-off articulation* — names what they gave up to get something else
- *Tool-of-tools choice* — chooses or refuses scaffolds you offered, *and the explanation holds*
- *Plan-revision in response to evidence* — not executing the plan but updating it

**Proxies that look like agency but aren't:**

- Finishing fast (often avoidance, not ownership)
- Polished journal entries (often high conscientiousness + teacher-pleasing)
- Volunteering to help peers (sometimes agency, often social positioning)
- Confident verbal pitches (rehearsed performance ≠ owned thinking)
- Dutiful kanban use (compliance theatre wearing agency's clothes)

The diagnostic I'd build into the unit: **three Cs — choice, causation, change.** Did the student choose? Can they explain *why*? Did the choice change their next action? If only one or two are present, it's not yet agency.

## 2. Project management for 12-13 year olds

**Kanban is the right call.** The visual-spatial nature suits the developmental stage; the explicit "doing" column externalises working memory; the to-do/doing/done structure maps cleanly onto the design cycle. The practitioner literature — eduScrum (Willy Wijnands), Agile Classrooms (John Miller), the broader Agile-in-Education community — has worked with this age group for over a decade. It's not peer-reviewed at scale, but practitioner convergence is strong.

**WIP limits with adolescents:** no direct empirical literature I'm aware of, but the cognitive science is favourable. Sweller's cognitive load theory and Monsell's task-switching research both predict that low WIP would help adolescents disproportionately, because (a) working memory is still consolidating and (b) adolescents systematically *underestimate* switching costs (consistent with the planning-fallacy work, Buehler, Griffin & Ross, 1994). Practical: a WIP limit of 1 in Doing per student. Sell it as "finish before you start."

**Forward vs backward — both, in a hybrid.** Backward planning teaches *project consciousness* (the deliverable is real and dated). Forward planning teaches *next-action discipline*. The research-backed synthesis: Wiggins & McTighe's *Understanding by Design* and Goldratt's Critical Chain support backward decomposition; Dawson & Guare's *Smart but Scattered* and the executive-function literature say next-action visibility is what drives adolescent execution. Use both: **set 3-4 backward milestones from June 11 in week 1**, then **forward-plan in single-class chunks**, re-anchoring to milestones each Friday.

**Blockage triage transfers well.** The agile standup — *did, will do, blocked* — is one of the most robust patterns available, and it's pure metacognition. The "blocked" question matters most: it normalises help-seeking and surfaces the difference between "I can't" and "I haven't started." Run it as a 5-minute opener, not a closer.

**One addition I'd push hard for: a time-estimation loop.** Have students estimate how long a card will take *before* moving it to Doing, then track actuals. After ~6 cards they'll see their own bias. Estimation calibration is one of the highest-leverage agency-adjacent meta-skills available in PM, and it costs almost nothing.

## 3. Process journal — getting beyond performative reflection

The default 3-prompt structure (did / decide / next) is solid scaffolding but lives mostly at **Hatton & Smith's (1995) "descriptive reflection"** level — one rung above pure description, three rungs below "critical reflection." For this age and duration (14 entries), I'd push for a 4-prompt structure that engineers a *surprise* moment:

- **Did** — what you actually did, not what you planned
- **Noticed** — what surprised you, broke, or didn't go as expected
- **Decided** — what you changed your mind about
- **Next** — your next move, and why *that* and not something else

The pivot is "noticed." Research on professional vision (van Es & Sherin) and on reflective practice (Schön's *reflection-in-action*) consistently finds that **noticing the unexpected is the doorway to learning**. Without it, journals collapse into chronicles. Berger's *An Ethic of Excellence* and the EL Education tradition make the same point in maker terms — students who can't name what surprised them aren't yet seeing their work.

**Photo as anchor — keep it, but make it specific.** Ask for a photo of *one decision point* per entry, not "a photo of your work." Reggio Emilia's documentation pedagogy treats the image as a third teacher; in your context, photo-of-decision forces the entry to be about something concrete and prevents abstraction-laundering.

**Audience matters more than prompts.** If students write only for the teacher, you'll get teacher-pleasing prose. Three patterns shift this:

- Pair-share before submitting (Costa & Kallick's Critical Friends protocols)
- Selective public sharing (one entry per week chosen by the student goes on a wall or feed)
- Periodic *self*-rereading — "read your last three entries, what's the pattern?"

That last one is the highest-leverage intervention here. Self-rereading is what makes a journal a tool of agency rather than an assignment.

## 4. Self-assessment frequency — signal vs noise

**Not every class. Almost certainly not.** Self-report fatigue in adolescents is well-documented: ratings flatten, students develop response sets (always pick 4), the act becomes ritualised. Experience-sampling-method literature and self-concept measurement work (Marsh & O'Mara) both find diminishing returns past weekly cadence for trait-like constructs.

For a 14-class unit I'd recommend **5 ratings** — classes 1, 4, 7, 11, 14 — spaced unevenly so students can't game the cadence. Baseline, three interim points (enough to see a trajectory), and a closing.

**But the deeper point: frequency is the wrong lever. Calibration is.** The metacognitive-accuracy literature (Nelson & Narens; the Dunning-Kruger work; more recently Heidi Andrade on student self-assessment) consistently finds that students get better at self-rating when ratings are *paired with external feedback and the gap is examined*. Rate once + calibrate against teacher view + discuss the discrepancy = more growth than rating five times alone.

Practical: pair the **classes 4 and 11 self-ratings with a teacher rating on the same rubric** and a 2-minute conversation about any large gap. That conversation is where the metacognition actually develops.

Hattie's effect sizes worth keeping in view: *self-reported grades / student expectations* sits near the top of his meta-analysis (d ≈ 1.3-1.4), but that effect measures predictive validity — students who can predict their grades accurately do better — not the effect of the act of rating. The intervention is the calibration, not the rating.

## 5. Hidden pitfalls — where this typically breaks

In rough order of likelihood in your context:

**Compliance theatre.** Students learn the *language* of agency ("I chose to…", "my next step is…") without the substance. The most common failure mode in middle-school agency work. Defence: insist on causal *because* clauses ("I chose X *because* Y, even though Z would have been easier") and refuse vague ones. The kanban is particularly vulnerable — students moving cards to look like they're doing PM rather than because PM is helping them.

**Teacher rescue.** The most insidious pitfall and the hardest to see in oneself. When a student is stuck, the teacher reflex is to help. Productive struggle is the *substrate* of agency — remove it, remove the thing the unit is supposed to develop. Discipline: a 10-minute rule before any teacher intervention; redirect to a peer or to the kanban first; ask diagnostic questions instead of giving solutions. The "Austin's Butterfly" video from EL Education is the canonical example of how *not* rescuing produces beautiful work.

**The race-day gravity well.** When a hard-dated external deliverable (the race) competes with the metacurriculum (agency, PM), the deliverable wins. Students will optimise for the car at the expense of the journal, the kanban, the reflection. Defence: assess agency and PM *more visibly* than the car. Make the metacurriculum the gradable thing. The race is celebration, not assessment.

**Cultural posture confounds in international-school China.** Specifically relevant for your context. Students from heritage cultures with strong adult-deference norms may rate themselves *low* not because of low agency but because self-promotion is culturally inappropriate; students fluent in Western self-presentation norms may rate themselves *high* without owning much. Triangulate: self-rating + observation + artifact evidence. Never let a self-rating stand alone.

**Agency-rich get richer.** Students with high prior agency thrive in open structures and disappear from your radar (because they're fine). Low-agency students drift, and the openness that liberates the first group abandons the second. Defence: explicit attention rotation — disproportionate 1:1 time with the bottom third of agency observations, not the top.

**Group work hiding lurkers.** In maker projects, agency frequently gets carried by 1-2 students per group. The car gets built; one student's name is on the kanban; the others are bystanders with credit. Defence: individual journals, individual kanban cards, an early "what's the division of labour?" prompt that makes invisible work visible.

**Tool fetish.** The kanban becomes the work. The journal becomes the work. The platform becomes the work. The *cars* become the work. Each displaces the learning goal. Defence: name this risk explicitly with students in week 1 — "these tools serve your thinking; when they stop serving it, tell me." That instruction is itself an agency move.

**Patterns from the named schools worth borrowing:**

- **High Tech High / EL Education** — *critique* is their agency lever. "Kind, specific, helpful" critique protocols, multiple drafts, public exhibition. For your unit: a critique session at the midpoint where students give peers structured feedback on a *decision* they made, not just on the artifact.
- **Big Picture Learning** — the *advisory* — a long-term mentoring relationship — is their core mechanism. You don't have advisor scaffolding, but the analogue is: 4-5 students per class for a 5-minute weekly 1:1, rotating so every student has at least two of these in the unit.
- **NuVu** — the *studio model* — no grades during the studio, just iteration, then a final review. The transferable move: **delay summative judgment**. Make the formative period genuinely formative; resist marking until the end.
- **Agency by Design (Project Zero)** — the *thinking routines*, especially *Parts/Purposes/Complexities* applied to their own car and *Imagine If* applied to a design decision. Ten-minute interventions that move the journal from description into design thinking.

If I had to pick one move from across all of this: **make the meta-curriculum more visible and more assessed than the artifact**. The race is the joy. The agency and the PM are the unit. Everything you do in StudioLoom should reinforce that ordering — whatever you build for kanban and journals should *outweigh* whatever you build for the car itself in the gradebook and in the visual dashboard students see.

# Gemini response — CO2 Racers agency unit research

This is a brilliant pedagogical pivot. Moving from "product-oriented" to "process-and-agency-oriented" in a familiar context (since they already know the build pipeline) is the perfect way to isolate and teach Project Management and Agency. Because cognitive load isn't maxed out on learning how to use Onshape or shape balsa, they have the bandwidth to learn how to manage themselves.

Here is a deep dive into the pedagogical research, frameworks, and practical patterns to help you sharpen this unit.

## 1. Research on Adolescent Agency: SDT & Observable Behaviors

**The Theory:** Self-Determination Theory (SDT) by Ryan and Deci posits that intrinsic motivation (agency) requires three psychological needs: Autonomy, Competence, and Relatedness. At 12-13, adolescents are experiencing a neurological shift where peer relatedness and autonomy become hyper-salient, while executive function (prefrontal cortex) is still highly malleable.

**Agency in a Maker Context:** Project Zero's Agency by Design framework (Harvard GSE) defines this as "Maker Empowerment"—a sensitivity to the designed world and the capacity to shape it.

**Observable Evidence of Agency:**

- *Resourcefulness:* Seeking out a peer, a YouTube tutorial, or a reference model before raising a hand to ask the teacher.
- *Pivoting after failure:* Looking at a botched 3D print, diagnosing the issue (e.g., "needs supports"), and adjusting the file without being told to do so.
- *Tool selection:* Choosing the right tool for the job based on the material, rather than just using whatever is closest.
- *Boundary pushing:* Asking, "Can I modify the standard wheel design to make it lighter?"

**Proxy Behaviors (Compliance Theatre):**

- *The "Is this right?" loop:* Executing a step perfectly, but refusing to move to the next step without teacher validation. (This is high competence, low autonomy).
- *Performative busyness:* Sanding the same piece of wood for 40 minutes to look busy while avoiding the cognitively harder task of CAD modeling.
- *Following the rubric as a checklist:* Doing exactly the minimum required to get the grade, rather than engaging with the design problem.

## 2. Project Management for Adolescents: Frameworks & Flow

**The Framework:** EduScrum (an adaptation of Agile/Scrum for education) and Kanban are highly effective here.

**WIP (Work In Progress) Limits:** Research on adolescent executive function shows that 12-13 year olds struggle with working memory and prioritization. WIP limits are essential. If a student has 5 things in "Doing," they are context-switching, not multitasking. Limit "Doing" to a maximum of 2 items. This forces completion before initiation—a critical PM skill.

**Blockage Triage:** The "Blocked" or "Waiting On" column is a game-changer. It teaches students to externalize their frustration. Instead of sitting idle, they move a sticky note (or digital card) to "Blocked" and must tag why (e.g., "Waiting for 3D printer," "Need teacher help with Onshape"). This transitions them from passive waiting to active management.

**Planning Direction:** You need a macro-backward, micro-forward approach.

- Macro: On Day 1, they must backward-map from June 11. "If the car must be painted and dry by June 10, when must sanding finish?" This builds temporal discounting awareness (a known weakness in middle schoolers).
- Micro: Daily planning should be forward-looking. "What are the 2 things I must move to 'Done' today to stay on track?"

## 3. Process Journal Design: Moving Beyond Performative Writing

The standard "What did you do / decide / next?" often yields: "I sanded my car. I decided it was smooth. Next I will paint." This is low-level recall, not reflection.

To get real reflection, look to Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle or the Stanford d.school's design thinking reflections. Shift the prompts to focus on friction, decisions, and evidence.

**Recommended 3-Prompt Structure:**

1. **The Friction (What happened):** "What was the biggest challenge or surprise you faced today, and how did you handle it?" (Forces them to acknowledge that design is not a linear, perfect process).
2. **The Evidence (Photo + Annotation):** "Upload a photo of your work today. Draw an arrow or write a caption pointing out one specific detail you are proud of or one flaw you need to fix." (Connecting visual evidence to critical analysis).
3. **The Pivot (Next steps):** "Based on today's progress, what is your #1 priority for next class, and what resources/tools will you need?" (Forces forward-planning and resource identification).

## 4. Self-Assessment Frequency: Signal vs. Noise

**The Research:** Experience Sampling Method (ESM) research shows that high-frequency surveying (every class) rapidly leads to survey fatigue, resulting in "straight-lining" (clicking the middle or top box just to get it over with).

- 14 times (Every class): Too much noise. It becomes a chore.
- 3 times (Start/Mid/End): Too sparse. If they rate themselves low on agency in the middle, they only have one more chance to fix it. It doesn't build a habit.
- The Sweet Spot (Milestone-based or Weekly): Tie self-assessment to natural project phases (e.g., End of Design Phase, End of CAD Phase, End of Build Phase). This usually maps to about 4 to 5 times across a 14-class unit.

**Alternative (Micro-daily + Macro-milestone):** If StudioLoom requires daily input, make the daily check-in a single, low-friction slider (e.g., "How focused were you today? 1-10"). Save the deep New Metrics Agency rubric (multi-dimensional self-assessment) for the 4-5 milestone days.

## 5. Hidden Pitfalls: Where Agency-as-Curriculum Fails

Schools like High Tech High and NuVu have documented the messy reality of shifting to agency. Here is where it usually falls apart:

**The Teacher Rescue:** A student is about to make a mistake (e.g., cutting a piece too short). The teacher steps in to stop them so their final car will look good. The fix: Let them make the non-dangerous mistakes. Agency is built in the recovery. If you rescue them, you teach them that you are the project manager.

**The "Tool over Task" Trap:** If updating the Kanban board or StudioLoom journal takes 15 minutes of a 50-minute class, students will resent it. PM tools must be ultra-efficient. The tool should serve the work, not the other way around.

**Undefined "Done":** Adolescents will move a task to "Done" when they are tired of doing it, not when it meets quality standards. The fix: Teach the Agile concept of "Definition of Done" (DoD). What makes a CAD file "Done"? (e.g., "Exported as STL, checked for manifold edges, uploaded to slicer").

**Grading the Reflection, Not the Honesty:** If students think they lose marks for admitting they wasted time or failed, they will lie in their process journals. You must explicitly grade the accuracy and depth of their self-awareness, rewarding students who say, "I got distracted today and accomplished nothing, so tomorrow I need to isolate myself from my friends."

By isolating the PM and Agency layer in a familiar physical build, you are giving them a sandbox to practice executive function. If StudioLoom can handle the scaffolding of the Kanban and the friction-based journaling, you will have a highly observable, measurable unit on student agency.

# Design Teaching Corpus — Layer 1 (Universal)
**Date:** 18 March 2026 | **Status:** Living document
**Purpose:** This is the AI's training material for what great design teaching looks like. Injected into system prompts for unit generation, lesson generation, and student mentoring. This is Layer 1 (universal) — framework-specific rules (Layer 2), school norms (Layer 3), and individual teacher style (Layer 4) override these when available.

**Important:** This document should be treated as a learning foundation, not rigid rules. As teachers upload their own lessons, provide feedback, and edit AI output, the system learns what works for THEM. These principles are starting points that get refined by real data.

---

## 1. The Design Cycle Is Non-Linear

Design education is fundamentally different from most subjects because the process is non-linear. Students don't move from step A to B to C to D in sequence — they jump, revisit, iterate, and sometimes start over. The AI must understand this:

- A student in the "Creating" phase might realise they need more research and loop back to "Inquiring"
- Evaluation happens constantly, not just at the end
- The design cycle diagram is a map, not a recipe — students take different paths through it
- Lesson plans that force a strict linear sequence are fighting the nature of the subject
- The best design teachers embrace the mess — they plan for non-linearity by building in decision points where students choose their next step

**IB MYP Design Cycle:** Inquiring & Analysing (A) → Developing Ideas (B) → Creating the Solution (C) → Evaluating (D) — but with constant loops between phases.

## 2. Lesson Phase Types

Design lessons use these recurring phase types. The AI should know when each is appropriate and how long it typically runs. Note: actual durations are learned from teacher uploads — these descriptions are for understanding purpose, not for setting fixed times.

### Warm-up / Vocabulary
Opens the lesson. Activates prior knowledge, introduces key terms, settles students. Keep brief. Can be a quick image analysis ("what do you see?"), a word matching exercise, or a physical object to handle.

### Direct Instruction / Demonstration
Teacher explains or demonstrates. The "I do" phase. In design, this is often a live demo at a workstation — showing a technique, walking through CAD software, explaining a process. Keep short for younger students. Always show, don't just tell. Use real materials and tools.

### Guided Practice
The "we do" phase. Students follow along with teacher guidance. In workshop settings, this means students practice the technique just demonstrated while the teacher circulates. Provide partially completed examples, templates, or step-by-step guides that scaffold the task.

### Independent Work / Making
The "you do" phase. Students work independently on their projects. This is where most design learning happens — through making, testing, failing, and iterating. The teacher's role shifts to circulating, asking questions, and providing targeted feedback. This phase can run long (30-50 min) because making is inherently engaging, but it needs checkpoints to prevent students drifting.

### Research / Investigation
Students gather information — online research, surveys, interviews, product analysis, material testing. Needs structure or students browse aimlessly. Provide guiding questions, research templates, or specific sources to analyse. Works best when students have a clear question to answer, not an open-ended "research your topic."

### Sketching / Ideation
Rapid idea generation through drawing, brainstorming, or design thinking tools. Quick bursts are better than long sessions — the energy comes from speed and volume. Techniques: thumbnail sketches, crazy 8s, SCAMPER, mind mapping. The goal is quantity over quality at this stage.

### Critique / Gallery Walk
Students give and receive feedback on work-in-progress. This is one of the most powerful learning activities in design education, but it needs explicit protocols or it degenerates into "looks good" / "I like it."

**Protocols that work:**
- **Warm/Cool feedback:** First say something warm (specific positive), then something cool (specific suggestion)
- **I Like / I Wish / What If:** Three structured sentences — "I like [specific thing], I wish [specific improvement], What if [creative suggestion]?"
- **Austin's Butterfly progression (Ron Berger):** Show iterative improvement through drafts — "here's draft 1, here's draft 2 after feedback, here's draft 3." Demonstrates that excellence comes from revision, not talent.
- **Silent gallery walk:** Students place work on tables, walk around in silence, leave sticky notes. Then discuss. Removes social pressure of face-to-face critique.
- **TAG feedback:** Tell something good, Ask a question, Give a suggestion.

**Critique rules:**
- Be Kind, Be Specific, Be Helpful (Ron Berger's three rules)
- Never criticise the person, only the work
- Comments must reference specific design elements, not general impressions
- "I like it" is not feedback. "I like how the handle curves to fit the palm" is feedback.

### Reflection / Journaling
Students process what they've learned. Best at end of lesson but can appear mid-lesson after significant activities. Keep brief (3-8 min). Can be verbal (pair share), written (exit ticket), or digital (portfolio entry).

**Effective reflection prompts:**
- "What worked? What didn't? What would you change?"
- "What did you learn from someone else's work today?"
- "What's the one thing you'll do differently next lesson?"
- "Rate your effort today 1-5 and explain why."

### Testing / Iteration
Students test their prototypes against success criteria, then modify. This is where the non-linear cycle is most visible — testing reveals problems that send students back to ideation or making. The teacher's role is to ask "what did you discover?" not "did it work?"

### Cleanup
Non-negotiable in workshop lessons. Must be scheduled explicitly — it doesn't "just happen." Students need clear routines: tools returned to specific locations, benches wiped, materials sorted, safety equipment checked. Time varies by equipment type.

## 3. The Gradual Release of Responsibility

Every skill in design education follows the "I do → We do → You do" progression:

1. **I do:** Teacher demonstrates the technique/process while students observe
2. **We do:** Students practice with scaffolding — step-by-step guides, templates, partial examples, teacher circulating
3. **You do:** Students apply independently — scaffolding is progressively removed

**In a workshop context, this looks like:**
- I do: Teacher demonstrates how to use the scroll saw (5-8 min)
- We do: Students practice cutting a simple shape with teacher supervision (10-15 min)
- You do: Students cut their own project pieces independently (20-40 min)

**The scaffolding removal is progressive, not sudden.** Common scaffolding tools in design:
- Checklists (step-by-step procedures)
- Sentence starters (for written reflections)
- Worked examples (completed project at each stage)
- Templates (partially completed sketches, pre-formatted documents)
- Exemplars (student work at different achievement levels)
- Graphic organisers (decision matrices, PMI charts, SCAMPER sheets)
- Peer support (pairing stronger with developing students)

## 4. Workshop Management Intelligence

### Noise Curves
Design workshops have a natural noise pattern. The AI should know this when designing lessons:
- **Quiet start:** Warm-up, demo, instruction = low noise
- **Controlled rise:** Guided practice = moderate noise (questions, collaboration)
- **Active peak:** Independent making = high noise (tools, discussion, movement)
- **Controlled drop:** Critique, reflection = noise drops back
- **Quiet end:** Cleanup routine, exit reflection = low noise

A lesson plan that puts the noisy activity last will fail because cleanup requires calm. The AI should sequence activities with the noise curve in mind.

### Material Distribution Patterns
- **Pre-set on benches** before students arrive (most efficient, requires prep time)
- **Central collection point** (students come to a table, collect what they need)
- **Teacher distributes** (slowest, but necessary for expensive or dangerous materials)
- **Station rotation** (materials already at each station, students move between them)

### Safety Consciousness
The AI must never generate a lesson involving tools or machines without considering:
- Is a safety brief needed? (Always yes for first use, reminder for repeat use)
- What PPE is required?
- What supervision ratio is needed?
- Are there age restrictions on any equipment?
- Is the cleanup procedure hazardous? (e.g., cleaning soldering irons, disposing of chemical waste)

## 5. "Playing the Whole Game" (David Perkins)

Design education is inherently a "whole game" — students are always working on a complete design challenge, not isolated sub-skills. The AI should generate units that maintain this wholeness:

- Every lesson should connect to the broader design challenge
- Students should always know WHY they're doing an activity (how it serves their project)
- Avoid "skill and drill" lessons disconnected from the project context
- Even when teaching a specific technique (e.g., soldering), contextualise it: "You're learning to solder because your circuit board needs these connections"

**Perkins' 7 Principles applied to design teaching:**
1. **Play the whole game** — students work on complete design challenges from day one
2. **Make the game worth playing** — use real problems, real users, real materials
3. **Work on the hard parts** — identify where students struggle and target practice there
4. **Play out of town** — transfer skills to new contexts (a student who learned empathy mapping for one project can apply it to another)
5. **Uncover the hidden game** — make design thinking strategies visible ("I'm choosing this material because...")
6. **Learn from the team** — peer critique, collaborative design, studio culture
7. **Learn the game of learning** — metacognition, self-assessment, reflection on process

## 6. Assessment in Design

### Process vs. Product
Design education uniquely values the process as much as (or more than) the final product. The AI should understand:
- A portfolio showing iterative improvement is more valuable than a perfect final product
- The journey matters — wrong turns, failures, and pivots demonstrate learning
- Documentation of the design process IS the assessment (not just the thing they made)

### Formative vs. Summative
- **Formative** (ongoing): Check-ins, exit tickets, peer critique, portfolio reviews, verbal conferences
- **Summative** (at milestones): Criterion-based assessment of completed work against rubric descriptors

### The Feedback Spectrum
How detailed and directive feedback should be depends on the design cycle phase:
- **Early stages (A: Inquiring):** More directive — guide students toward useful research questions and methods
- **Middle stages (B: Developing Ideas):** Balanced — encourage divergent thinking, then help narrow down
- **Making stages (C: Creating):** Less directive — ask questions rather than give answers ("What happens if you try...?")
- **Evaluation stages (D: Evaluating):** Prompt reflection — "How does this compare to your original intention?"

## 7. Studio Culture

The best design classrooms operate as studios, not traditional classrooms. The AI should generate content that fosters studio culture:

- **Work is always visible** — projects displayed at every stage, not just when "finished"
- **Critique is normal** — giving and receiving feedback is a daily practice, not a special event
- **Iteration is expected** — first drafts are first drafts, not final answers
- **Materials and tools are accessible** — students can independently get what they need
- **The teacher is a design mentor**, not a lecturer — moving between students, asking questions, demonstrating techniques on the spot
- **Student agency** — students make real decisions about materials, methods, timelines, and aesthetics
- **Beautiful work is the standard** (Ron Berger's ethic of excellence) — not perfection, but genuine effort toward quality

## 8. Differentiation in Design

Design naturally differentiates because:
- Students work at different paces on different projects
- There's no single "right answer" — multiple valid solutions exist
- Extension is built-in: "How can you make this better?" always works
- Scaffolding can be added/removed per student without changing the core task

**But the AI still needs to plan for:**
- ELL students who need simplified instructions and visual supports
- Students with fine motor difficulties who may need adapted tools or digital alternatives
- Advanced students who need deeper challenge, not more work
- Students who finish early (extension prompts, peer mentoring, or starting next phase)

## 9. Technology Integration

### Digital Tools
Design education uses technology as a tool, not the point. The AI should know when digital tools are appropriate:
- **CAD software** (TinkerCAD, Fusion 360, Onshape) — for 3D modelling, precision work, iteration without material waste
- **2D design** (Canva, Figma, Adobe Express) — for graphic design, packaging, presentation
- **Research tools** — curated databases, image search, patent databases, materials libraries
- **Documentation** — digital portfolios, blogs, video reflections
- **Prototyping** — laser cutting, 3D printing (these are prototyping tools, not production tools)

### When NOT to go digital
- Early sketching (paper is faster and less intimidating than CAD)
- Material exploration (students need to touch, bend, test real materials)
- Physical prototyping (cardboard models teach more than CAD renders about form and function)
- Critique sessions (physical work on tables > screenshots on screens)

## 10. How This Corpus Is Used

### In Unit Generation
The AI receives this corpus as context when generating unit skeletons and lessons. It informs:
- Phase sequencing (what follows what)
- Activity selection (which activities suit which learning goals)
- Scaffolding levels (matched to student age/ability)
- Assessment integration (formative checkpoints woven throughout)
- Workshop awareness (setup/cleanup, safety, materials)

### In Student Mentoring
The student-facing AI (Design Assistant) draws on this corpus to:
- Give feedback that's specific and actionable, not generic praise
- Ask questions that push thinking deeper (Socratic method informed by design pedagogy)
- Suggest next steps that align with good design practice
- Recognise and encourage iteration and revision

### In Knowledge Analysis
When analysing uploaded lesson plans, the AI uses this corpus as the standard to assess quality against — identifying strengths ("good use of gradual release") and gaps ("no critique protocol included").

### Learning and Evolution
This corpus evolves as the system learns:
- When teachers consistently upload lessons that contradict a principle here, the system notes the discrepancy
- When feedback data shows certain approaches work better, the corpus should be updated
- Different frameworks (IB MYP, GCSE, ACARA) may emphasise different principles — Layer 2 handles this
- Individual teachers override anything here with their own proven approaches — Layer 4 handles this
